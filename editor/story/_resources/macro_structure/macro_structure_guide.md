# macro_structure /: narrative-frameworks guide

*Originally a decoder for Ian Bousher's narrative-structure poster (~25 frameworks). Expanded with non-Western and abstract additions the poster omits (Nāṭyaśāstra, Ma, direct cinema, mosaic portrait, dual dramatic question, Arndt's Endings).*

*This is the human-readable companion to `editor/story/_resources/macro_structure/macro_structure_README.md`. The README documents the JSON schema; this guide is the essay: where each framework comes from, what it's for, and why most of them are secretly the same thing.*

---

## The one big idea

The chart's real argument is made by its layout, not its content. Every framework is stacked against the same horizontal Act I / II / III / IV spine, and once you read across, you see that the vast majority are **the same underlying shape described in different vocabularies**: a stable world is disrupted, a character commits to a goal, things escalate to a midpoint reversal, collapse into a low point, then resolve through a climax into a new equilibrium.

The differences between rows are mostly three things: (1) **resolution**: how finely they slice the spine (Aristotle gives you 3 beats, Truby gives you 22); (2) **discipline**: whether the author came from drama, anthropology, folklore, screenwriting, theology, or self-help; and (3) **engine**: whether the story is driven by *external conflict* (the dominant Western assumption) or by *change without conflict* (the East Asian counter-tradition).

The chart also quietly includes several frameworks that aren't narrative theory at all (grief, salvation, the scientific method). That's the deeper point: narrative structure is a pattern the mind imposes on *any* meaningful sequence, so storytellers can steal a shape from anywhere.

## Genealogy: precedents and antecedents

There are two great trunks and a few grafts.

**Trunk 1: the dramatic / conflict tradition.** This is the Western mainstream.
> Aristotle (*Poetics*, c. 335 BCE) → Horace's five acts (c. 19 BCE) → Donatus's protasis/epitasis/catastrophe (4th c.) → Freytag's pyramid (1863) → the modern screenwriting paradigm (Field 1979) → its prescriptive descendants (Snyder, McKee, Seger, Hauge, Wells, Duncan, Gulino).

**Trunk 2: the mythic / anthropological tradition.**
> Van Gennep's rites of passage (1909, the true source of separation–transition–return) and Frazer's *Golden Bough* + Jung's archetypes → Campbell's monomyth (1949) → Vogler's screenwriting adaptation (1985/1992) → its simplifications (Harmon, Watts). Propp (1928) is the formalist cousin: same folktale material, structuralist method instead of mythic.

**The graft: borrowed shapes.** Kübler-Ross (grief), Boston/Augustine (salvation), Kishōtenketsu (East Asian four-act), and the scientific method are imported from outside storytelling. Yorke (2013) is the late synthesizer who argues all of the above collapse into one fractal five-act pattern.

The honest lineage caveat: the screenwriting gurus rarely cite Aristotle directly, and most of them reinvented the same beats independently from watching films. The chart makes the convergence look like descent. It's closer to parallel evolution toward the same attractor.

## The shared spine (how they intersect)

The clearest way to see the convergence is to map the major beat-based systems onto one percentage spine. Read each column top to bottom and the rows are near-synonyms.

| Position | ~Stable world | Inciting incident (~10%) | End of Act I (~25%) | Midpoint (~50%) | Low point / "all is lost" (~75%) | Climax (~90%) | New equilibrium |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Aristotle** | Beginning | (complication begins) | - | - | - | Reversal + recognition | End |
| **Freytag** | Exposition | Exciting force | Rising action | *Climax* (apex)\* | Falling action | Catastrophe | Denouement |
| **Field** | Set-Up | Inciting incident | Plot Point 1 | Midpoint | Plot Point 2 | Climax | Resolution |
| **Snyder** | Opening image / setup | Catalyst | Break Into Two | Midpoint | All Is Lost → Dark Night | Finale (Break Into Three) | Final image |
| **McKee** | Exposition | Inciting incident | (complications) | (complications) | Crisis (decision) | Climax | Resolution |
| **Vogler** | Ordinary World | Call to Adventure | Crossing the Threshold | Ordeal | The Road Back | Resurrection | Return with Elixir |
| **Campbell** | Ordinary World | Call | Crossing 1st Threshold | Belly of the Whale | Refusal of Return | Master of Two Worlds | Freedom to Live |
| **Harmon** | Comfort zone (You) | Need | Cross threshold (Go) | Find it | Pay the price | Return | Changed |
| **Hauge** | Setup | Opportunity | Change of Plans | Point of No Return | Major Setback | Climax | Aftermath |

\* **The one real disagreement:** Freytag puts the *climax* at the structural center (apex of the pyramid) and calls the late catastrophe something else. Modern usage moved "climax" to ~90%. So when a film teacher and a literature professor argue about where the climax is, they're often just using Freytag vs. Field definitions. Knowing the difference keeps you from talking past your editor.

## The frameworks, by family

### 1. Classical foundations

**Aristotle** *(Poetics, c. 335 BCE)*. The root. Plot (*mythos*) is supreme over character; a story needs a beginning, middle, and end with unity of action and causal necessity. His engine is *peripeteia* (reversal) plus *anagnorisis* (recognition) producing *catharsis*. He also split plot into *desis* (complication/tying) and *lysis* (denouement/untying), which is the literal ancestor of "rising action / falling action."

**Horace** *(Ars Poetica, c. 19 BCE)*. Prescriptive Roman rule: a play should have "neither less nor more than five acts." This is why five-act structure (and later Yorke) keeps recurring. Less a theory than a convention that hardened into law.

**Aelius Donatus** *(4th c. CE)*. Roman grammarian whose commentary on Terence divided comedy into **protasis** (exposition), **epitasis** (rising complication), and **catastrophe** (resolution). Renaissance critics later inserted *catastasis*. This is the three-act idea in embryo, predating Field by 1,600 years.

**Gustav Freytag** *(Die Technik des Dramas, 1863)*. The Pyramid: introduction, rising action, climax, falling action ("return or fall"), catastrophe. Built to describe five-act Classical and Shakespearean tragedy, *not* contemporary film, which is why its symmetry (equal fall after the central climax) fits almost no modern movie. Useful as a tragedy lens; misleading as a screenwriting template.

**Kishōtenketsu** *(classical Chinese/Japanese/Korean, from 起承転結 qǐ-chéng-zhuǎn-hé)*. The important outlier. Four parts: **ki** (introduction), **shō** (development), **ten** (a *twist*: an apparently unrelated new element), **ketsu** (reconciliation that retroactively unites the parts). **No conflict is required.** Its presence on the chart is the strongest evidence that conflict-and-resolution is a Western cultural convention rather than a law of storytelling. Directly relevant to documentary, where forcing a villain onto real events can feel false.

### 2. Myth, folklore, and the monomyth branch

**Vladimir Propp** *(Morphology of the Folktale, 1928)*. Russian Formalist who analyzed ~100 Russian fairy tales and extracted **31 narrative functions** (absentation, interdiction, violation, villainy/lack, departure, the donor sequence, struggle, victory, return, recognition, etc.) and **7 character roles** (hero, villain, donor, helper, princess, dispatcher, false hero). Philosophy: the *sequence of functions* is stable even when the surface content changes. He's the scientific, bottom-up counterpart to Campbell's mystical, top-down monomyth, and a direct ancestor of computational/structuralist story analysis.

**Joseph Campbell** *(The Hero with a Thousand Faces, 1949)*. The monomyth: 17 stages in three movements, **Departure, Initiation, Return**. Synthesized from Jung (archetypes, the collective unconscious), Frazer, and especially Van Gennep's rites of passage. Philosophy: all myths express one universal psychological journey of transformation. Hugely influential, also fairly criticized for cherry-picking and for flattening cultural specificity.

**Christopher Vogler** *(The Writer's Journey, 1992; originally a 7-page Disney memo, c. 1985)*. The practical translation of Campbell into 12 screenwriting beats. This is the version that actually runs Hollywood: it shaped the Disney Renaissance and the modern blockbuster. Where Campbell is descriptive and anthropological, Vogler is prescriptive and production-ready.

**Dan Harmon** *(Story Circle, c. 2009)*. An eight-step radical simplification of Campbell for television, built for fast, repeatable episode breaking on *Community* and *Rick and Morty*: a character is in a **comfort** zone, **wants** something, **enters** an unfamiliar situation, **adapts**, **gets** it, **pays** a price, **returns**, having **changed**. Its genius is compression: it's the monomyth you can hold in your head.

**Nigel Watts** *(Writing a Novel and Getting Published, 1996)*. The **Eight-Point Arc**: stasis, trigger, quest, surprise, critical choice, climax, reversal, resolution. A clean, novel-oriented restatement of the same spine; the "critical choice" emphasis (character must actively decide, not be swept along) is its most teachable contribution.

### 3. Modern screenwriting paradigms

**Syd Field** *(Screenplay, 1979)*. The foundational modern text. The **Paradigm**: three acts (Set-Up / Confrontation / Resolution) hinged on two **Plot Points**, later refined with a **Midpoint** and two **Pinches**. Tied to page count (≈120 pages = 120 minutes; Plot Point I near p.25, Plot Point II near p.85). Everything prescriptive after this is a reaction to or elaboration of Field.

**Paul Gulino** *(Screenwriting: The Sequence Approach, 2004)*. Argues the three-act model is too coarse and that films are really built from **eight 10–15 minute sequences**, each a mini-movie with its own tension and partial resolution. A legacy of the silent-era practice of shipping films on physical reels. The most useful corrective to "what do I do in the sagging middle of Act II."

**Robert McKee** *(Story, 1997)*. The philosophical heavyweight. Spine: inciting incident upsets the balance of life; the protagonist conceives an object of desire and quests against escalating **forces of antagonism**; **progressive complications** lead to a **crisis** (the ultimate decision under maximum pressure), then **climax** and **resolution**. Core concept: the **gap** between expectation and result is where meaning is generated. Conflict is non-negotiable for McKee, which is exactly where Kishōtenketsu pushes back.

**Linda Seger** *(Making a Good Script Great, 1987)*. From the development/script-doctor side rather than the auteur side. Three acts, two turning points, a clear central question, and an emphasis on **rewriting** as the real work. Where McKee is theory, Seger is diagnostics: how to find and fix what's broken.

**Blake Snyder** *(Save the Cat!, 2005)*. The most prescriptive and most commercially dominant. A **15-beat sheet** with literal page numbers (Opening Image p.1, Theme Stated p.5, Catalyst p.12, Break Into Two p.25, Midpoint p.55, All Is Lost p.75, etc.) plus the "save the cat" likability moment and a strong **B-story** (usually the relationship/theme line). Loved for its usability, blamed for formulaic sameness. The "B-story" idea is worth stealing for any doc with parallel arcs.

**Michael Hauge** *(Writing Screenplays That Sell, 1988)*. **Six-Stage Plot Structure** (Setup, New Situation, Progress, Complications & Higher Stakes, Final Push, Aftermath) hinged on five turning points (Opportunity 10%, Change of Plans 25%, Point of No Return 50%, Major Setback 75%, Climax 90%). His real contribution is the **two parallel journeys**: the outer plot goal *and* the inner arc from **living in your Identity (the false self / mask)** to **living your Essence (your truth)**. The cleanest articulation of "external plot vs. internal transformation."

**John Truby** *(The Anatomy of Story, 2007)*. The deliberate anti-template. **22 steps** organized around the character's **moral and psychological need**, not act breaks: ghost/weakness, need, desire, opponent, plan, drive, apparent defeat, second revelation, **self-revelation**, moral decision, new equilibrium. Philosophy: structure should grow *organically* from the protagonist's desire line and moral argument; rigid 3-act counting kills stories. The richest framework for character-driven and ensemble work; the hardest to apply fast.

**Dan Wells** *(Seven-Point Story Structure, lectures c. 2011)*. Hook, Plot Turn 1, Pinch 1, Midpoint, Pinch 2, Plot Turn 2, Resolution. Explicitly derived from Lester Dent's pulp-fiction master formula. Distinctive method: **plot backward from the resolution** so the hook is the mirror-image of the ending. Excellent for ensuring your beginning and end rhyme.

**Steve Duncan** *(A Guide to Screenwriting Success, 2006)*. A film-and-TV professor's variant tracking three parallel columns (premise/goal, the protagonist's psychological state, and plot function) through a quest-shaped arc: brave new world, gaining skills, pushed to the limit, on the run, epiphany, final confrontation. Notable for explicitly handling **television** alongside features.

**John Yorke** *(Into the Woods, 2013)*. The synthesizer and the most intellectually satisfying row. A BBC drama executive who argues every story is a **fractal five-act structure** of **change**: the protagonist moves from no-knowledge through doubt, reluctance, and regression to reawakening and mastery, and the same shape recurs at the level of the whole film, the act, the scene, and the beat. His move is to absorb all the other rows into one pattern, with change (not conflict, not the hero's journey) as the engine.

### 4. Heuristics and aphorisms

**"Put your character up a tree, throw rocks at him, get him down."** A folk writing maxim (variously attributed; no reliable single source). The poster shows two variants, including a "set the tree on fire" escalation. It's the entire dramatic tradition compressed to a joke: establish a goal, escalate obstacles, resolve. The "set the tree on fire" version is just McKee's progressive complications with a punchline.

**Kurt Vonnegut: "Shapes of Stories"** *(from his rejected University of Chicago anthropology master's thesis; popularized in lectures)*. Plots graphed as lines on a good-fortune/ill-fortune axis: **Man in Hole** (gets in trouble, gets out, ends better), **Boy Meets Girl** (finds something wonderful, loses it, regains it), **Cinderella** (a step-rise, sudden collapse, then off-the-chart happiness), **From Bad to Worse**. Philosophy: emotional *trajectory* matters more than event taxonomy. The most useful lens for documentary because it's about felt shape, not plot mechanics.

**The Scientific Method** (problem → research → hypothesis → experiment → analysis → conclusion). A semi-tongue-in-cheek inclusion making the chart's thesis explicit: inquiry has the same dramatic shape as story (a question disrupts equilibrium, investigation escalates, a finding resolves it). Genuinely useful framing for investigative and "uncovering the truth" documentaries.

### 5. Borrowed shapes (non-narrative frameworks repurposed)

**Elisabeth Kübler-Ross: Five Stages of Grief** *(On Death and Dying, 1969)*. Denial, anger, bargaining, depression, acceptance (the chart adds an initiating "trauma"). Not a story theory at all; it's a clinical model of dying that writers co-opted as a ready-made **emotional arc** for a character processing loss. Kübler-Ross herself never meant it as linear or universal, which is the standard caution.

**Thomas Boston: the Fourfold State** *(Human Nature in Its Fourfold State, 1720)* and the **Augustinian redemptive-historical** row beside it. Boston's four states: **Primitive Integrity** (innocence), **Entire Depravity** (the fall), **Begun Recovery** (grace), **Consummate Happiness** (glory). The adjacent Latin row (*posse peccare / non posse peccare* and Creation–Fall–Salvation–Eternity) is **Augustine's four states of human will**, the spine of the entire Christian metanarrative. Their point on the chart: the Bible's macro-structure is itself a four-act story (paradise, loss, redemption, restoration), which is why redemption arcs feel archetypal.

**The stage-musical two-act** (Normal World → Inciting Incident → Point of No Return → **Intermission** → Midpoint resumes → "The Big Gloom" → Climax into Resolution → New Normal). A theatrical-exhibition convention: structure bent around the practical need for an interval, with a deliberate Act-I-closing "button" and an Act-II "11 o'clock number" low point. Form following the building, not the story.

### 6. Beyond the poster: additions for documentary work

The poster is a great map of *Western fiction screenwriting*. For a documentary, especially one with ensemble subjects and observed material, three things are missing or under-developed: (a) traditions outside the conflict-engine paradigm, (b) frameworks that handle ensembles rather than single protagonists, and (c) endings-specific diagnostics that catch the failure mode where the plot resolves but the meaning doesn't. The frameworks below fill those gaps.

**Nāṭyaśāstra: the five sandhis** *(attributed to Bharata Muni, c. 200 BCE–200 CE)*. The foundational Sanskrit treatise on dramaturgy. Predates Aristotle's *Poetics* and developed independently. Five "sandhis" or junctures: **Mukha** (opening), **Pratimukha** (progression), **Garbha** (the womb, the deep middle), **Vimarśa** (reflection), **Nirvahana** (conclusion). But the engine isn't conflict. It's **rasa**: the cultivation of a dominant aesthetic emotion in the audience. A film built on Nāṭyaśāstra principles isn't asking "what's at stake?"; it's asking "what flavor am I producing?" Documentaries with meditative or contemplative passages have Nāṭyaśāstra DNA whether the editor knows it or not. The Garbha juncture is particularly useful for thinking about the long Act II middle as gestation rather than struggle.

**Ma: negative space as structure** *(classical Japanese aesthetic; 間)*. Not a stage framework at all. The principle that **meaning lives in the gaps between events as much as in the events themselves**. Ozu's pillow shots between scenes; Tarkovsky's sustained holds; the silence after a verdict before anyone speaks. Most Western frameworks treat the space between beats as transition to be minimized; ma treats it as content to be cultivated. For documentary, ma is the answer to Vonnegut's "earn the descent" caution: letting the audience SIT in the despair (or the awe, or the loss) is the concrete craft move that lets the next beat land. The macro filter records the principle; the micro-principles family `rhythm_pacing` gives the actual editing-room moves.

**Direct cinema: observational mode** *(Maysles brothers, Frederick Wiseman, D.A. Pennebaker, Robert Drew; 1960s American documentary)*. Methodologically the opposite of the poster's frameworks: rejects imposed narrative structure in favor of observed reality. The editor's job is to find shape in what was filmed, not to retrofit Snyder beats onto it. "Letting the material speak for itself" is itself a position (adjacency creates meaning even without narration), but the position is that the FILMED EVENT, not a pre-decided story shape, is the source of meaning. Useful for documentary stretches where the subject has no pre-existing dramatic arc (training routines, friendships, daily life under stress). Less useful when the external world IS a drama (a trial, a campaign, a race); there the world's structure imposes itself whether the editor wants it or not.

**Mosaic portrait: ensemble without protagonist** *(implicit in Koyaanisqatsi, Faces Places, Manakamana; theorized by Bill Nichols, Michael Renov)*. The single-protagonist arc is so dominant in Western storytelling that ensemble pieces tend to get retrofitted as "groups of protagonists." Mosaic portrait does the opposite: many small tiles (a vignette, an interaction, a portrait) accumulate into a collective picture that has shape but no single arc. Documentaries about communities, places, and processes are mosaic structures even when their editors don't name them as such. Treating an ensemble's members as Vogler-style allies of one protagonist flattens their individual arcs and makes the supporting cast feel like instrumentality.

**Dual dramatic question: two stacked goals, resolved in reverse** *(implicit in many ensemble films; closest theoretical antecedent in Snyder's A-story/B-story but with both stories treated as substantively external)*. **Problem A** is the global/systemic question; **Problem B** is the local/character question. The protagonist pursues B; in pursuing B they encounter A; resolving B sets the conditions for resolving A. The "canoe toward the waterfall" Act-II break is the moment B's local urgency reveals A's global stakes. Different from Snyder's B-story because B isn't a relationship subplot; it's a substantive external goal in its own right.

**Michael Arndt, Endings: The Three-Climax Architecture** *(lecture/essay c. 2014; Arndt wrote* Little Miss Sunshine, Toy Story 3, *and shares story credit on* The Force Awakens). The most actionable diagnostic for Act III. Argues great endings detonate **three climaxes in rapid sequence**: (1) **External**: the protagonist achieves or definitively fails their stated goal; (2) **Internal**: the protagonist resolves the wound or lack that was hidden from them at the start; (3) **Philosophical**: an antagonist who embodied an opposing worldview is defeated, surrendered, or transformed, and with them, the worldview itself. The "insanely great" ending fires all three in the same moment. Arndt's secondary claim: at the crisis, the protagonist often **rejects the external goal** in service of the internal need, and the rejection IS the win (Andy gives away the toys; Olive doesn't compete for the pageant the way the pageant wants her to). The diagnostic value: ask of any Act III, "is this one climax, or three?" If one, the ending is plot; if three, the ending is meaning.

**Comedy structure: modes, engines, and the dramatic inversion** *(Northrop Frye's* Anatomy of Criticism, *1957; modern instantiations from Lubitsch, Hawks, Christopher Guest, Adam McKay, Last Week Tonight, Mel Brooks, Phoebe Waller-Bridge)*. The poster ignores comedy almost entirely (it's a story-shape chart focused on dramatic conflict), so this filter fills the gap. Comedy is a STRUCTURAL mode, not a vibe. It inverts the dramatic engine: instead of antagonist vs. protagonist, the engine is **foolishness vs. virtue** or **rigidity vs. flexibility**. Comedy's ending is restoration / marriage / community renewed, not the dramatic ending of dragon-slain / hero-transformed-alone. Seven sub-modes covered in the filter:

  - **Old Comedy** (Aristophanes: political satire targeting power; modern echoes in Last Week Tonight)
  - **New Comedy** (Menander → Plautus: boy meets girl, obstacles, marriage)
  - **Romantic Comedy** (Shakespeare: green-world transformation)
  - **Comedy of Manners** (Restoration → Wilde → Whit Stillman: social rules generating comedy)
  - **Dark Comedy** (Kubrick, Coens: laughs in proximity to grief; e.g. *Strangelove*, *Fargo*)
  - **Dramatic Comedy** (Apatow, Payne, Adam McKay: serious stakes with comic delivery; e.g. *Sideways*, *The Big Short*, *Don't Look Up*)
  - **Meta-Subversive Comedy** (Brecht → Mel Brooks → Monty Python → *Ferris Bueller* → *Scream* → *Community* → *Fleabag* → Deadpool: comedy where the FORM itself is part of the joke). Three intensities on a single dial: **light** (Mel Brooks acknowledgment-wink the audience enjoys), **medium** (Fleabag-style sustained fourth-wall complicity, requires a designated narrator-figure), **heavy** (Deadpool full genre-violation). The intensity is the editorial choice; the mode is the same.
