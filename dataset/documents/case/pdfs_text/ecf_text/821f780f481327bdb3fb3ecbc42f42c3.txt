BRIEF IN SUPPORT OF MOTION TO DISMISS

Defendant Michelino Sunseri respectfully requests this Court dismiss the

violation notice filed against him (ECF 1) with prejudice. This motion is brought

pursuant to Fed. R. of Crim. P. (FRCrP) 12(b)(1), (3)(A) asserting (A) failure to

state an offense; violation of Defendant's Fifth Amendment rights to (B) equal

protection and (C) due process; and (D) violation of U.S. const. Art. I. § 1 for

unconstitutional delegation of legislative power.

In support thereof, Defendant states as follows:

I. INTRODUCTION

1. Defendant was cited under 36 CFR § 2.1(b) for using an allegedly closed

social trail in Grand Teton National Park on Sep. 2, 2024 (ECF 1).

2. The Defendant seeks dismissal based on: (A) insufficient evidence of a

valid closure under 36 CFR §§ 2.1(b), 1.5 and 1.7; (B) selective

enforcement violating equal protection; (C) inadequate notice violating

due process; and (D) unconstitutional delegation of legislative power.

3. Under FRCrP 12(b)(3)(B)(v), a charge may be dismissed for failure to state

an offense where the alleged facts do not establish a violation. See U.S. v.

Pope, 613 F.3d 1255, 1259 (10th Cir. 2010).

4. Dismissal is also warranted where enforcement violates constitutional

rights, including due process and equal protection under the Fifth

Amendment (see U.S. v. Armstrong, 517 U.S. 456, 464 (1996)), or the

nondelegation doctrine under Article I § 1 (see Touby v. U.S., 500 U.S.

160, 165-66 (1991) ("something more than an 'intelligible principle'" may

be required when criminal sanctions are implicated)).

II. BACKGROUND

5. Defendant used a widely-traveled social trail on Sep. 2, 2024, while

pursuing a speed record ascending and descending Grand Teton. On

Strava—from which the government also retrieved data in this case—at

least 57 users posted 83 public runs using the trail (ECF 32-2). Despite

consistent, ongoing public use, Defendant is not aware of any other

citations for use of the old climber's trail.

6. The old climber's trail is incomparable to other sensible applications of

36 CFR § 2.1(b)—e.g., boardwalks in Yellowstone's West Thumb Geyser

Basin protecting visitors from inadvertently falling into the Abyss Pool or

damaging delicate thermophiles, switchbacks descending from the Grand

Canyon's South Rim where shortcuts from one section to the next could

dislodge rocks onto hikers below.

7. To the contrary, the old climber's trail is a historically-significant, and

the original, trail into Garnet Canyon used for Grand Teton speed

records since the 1930s. As clearly evident from maps and photographs,

the trail is hard-packed and well-traveled with no trails below that would

expose other travelers to falling debris from its use.

8. Until at least Sep. 6, 2024, the Fastest Known Time website displayed

four accepted speed records using the old climber's trail without any

flags or warnings (ECF 32-4)—one accepted in 2022 with a public GPX

file (ECF 32-8 at 2). On Sep. 6, 2024, Ranger Bellino instructed staff of

Fastest Known Time—a private website owned by Outside Inc.—to reject

Defendant's speed record (ECF 32-8 at 2).

9. Before Defendant's citation, third-party maps showed the trail as open,

akin to the popular, nearby Delta Lake social trail (which has over eleven

thousand reviews (AllTrails.com)). Off-trail travel is broadly permitted in

National Parks, as clarified in the 1983 rulemaking intent behind 36 CFR

§ 2.1(b). See 48 FR 30263 (Jun. 30, 1983).

10. The government's only reference to alleged interest in a citation for use of

the old climber's trail related to the most recent men's speed record

using that trail: Kilian Jornet in 2012. (ECF 32-8 at 2). A public YouTube

video with 711K views published by Anton Krupicka, a resident of

Colorado, shows Anton and Kilian running on the old climber's trail

during that trip (ECF 32-7). Anton and Kilian were never charged.

11. On Sep. 11, 2024, after Defendant learned from Fastest Known Time

staff of a potential criminal investigation, the Defendant reached out to

Grand Teton NPS via Instagram expressing his surprise and offering his

volunteer efforts to properly close the trail (ECF 32-8 at 2):

"I recently ran a speed record on [G]rand Teton and took the switchback on the way down. I was hoping to talk to someone about making sure this doesn't happen again by helping out with closing the specific trail. I love the parks and deeply respect what everyone who works in the park does. These are important places to me and I want to set a good example for anyone in the future. I've heard rumblings from Fastest Known Time that the [NPS] plans on pressing charges against me. This is quite alarming as I was under the assumption this trail was okay due to the Fastest Known Time's website posting of other [athletes] who have used this route. After my attempt, they went back and changed the rules [. . .] I'm hoping to talk to someone about rectifying this situation."

12. On Sep. 12, 2024, a ranger called Defendant noting that the NPS

received his message and would be in touch. On Sep. 25, 2024, Ranger

Bellino contacted the Defendant, joined by Ranger Michelle Altizer (ECF

32-8 at 2). At trial Defendant is prepared to testify as to the following: on

that call, Defendant asked Bellino, "What's the scope of this call?" to

which Bellino replied, "We want to drag you in front of a judge and make

you apologize"—ignoring Defendant's prior efforts to "[rectify] this

situation" and forcing involvement of counsel.

13. Following the Sep. 25, 2024, conversation with Bellino, Defendant was

charged with a Federal misdemeanor for running on an allegedly closed

trail and threatened with "a fine of up to $5,000 or six months in jail" by

an NPS spokesperson in press comments (ECF 32-6 at 2).

14. According to the Federal Rulemaking behind 36 CFR § 2.1(b), such

restrictions are "not intended to prohibit cross-country travel, or off-trail

exploring." 48 FR 30263. The NPS explicitly allow many activities that

require off-trail travel in similar, or more fragile and erosion-prone,

terrain: climbing (https://nps.gov/grte/planyourvisit/climb.htm), elk

hunting (https://nps.gov/grte/planyourvisit/elkreduction.htm), wildlife

viewing (https://nps.gov/grte/planyourvisit/wildview.htm), and fishing

(https://nps.gov/grte/planyourvisit/fish.htm).

15. NPS representatives further elaborated in public statements that the

decision to press charges was motivated by: Defendant's (1) identity as

an "influencer and sponsored athlete," (2) "effort to achieve the fastest

known time," and (3) related public race report on Strava and Instagram

post with sponsor, The North Face, with photographs from the film crew

documenting the feat (ECF 32-6 at 2; ECF 32-8 corroborates)—activities

protected under U.S. const. amend. I. and V.

16. All plea offers to date have included a five-year ban from Grand Teton

National Park, which would force the Defendant—a local bartender and

professional mountain runner who regularly trains in the Tetons—to

move from his home in Victor, ID.

III. ARGUMENT

A. Invalid Closure Under 36 CFR § 2.1(b)

17. To sustain a violation of 36 CFR § 2.1(b), the government must prove the

trail was closed under 36 CFR §§ 1.5 (written determination) and 1.7

(notice). 36 CFR § 2.1(b) is not intended to prevent "off-trail exploring,"

(48 FR 30263), such as that permitted on the nearby Delta Lake trail.

18. Notice: Given the consistent public use of the old climber's trail without

citation (ECF 32-2) and historical use for speed records (ECF 32-4), 36

CFR § 1.7 requires far clearer closure notice than two small, earth-toned,

and ambiguous signs lacking CFR references, hidden in sagebrush and

behind a pine tree, respectively. ECF 21-1 at 9-10. The sign from the

direction of Defendant's entry merely states "Short cutting causes

erosion" and is located next to, not in front of, the old climber's trail,

hardly notice of a misdemeanor given the trail's consistent, uncited

public use. ECF 21-1 at 9. By contrast, the unofficial Delta Lake trail—

where the NPS explicitly allows heavy use of a social path within a mile of

the old climber's trail through nearly identical terrain—is marked by a

sign significantly larger and clearer than the alleged closure signs. See

AllTrails, Delta Lake via Lupine Meadows Access; ECF 21-1 at 9-10.

19. Written Determination: For any 36 CFR § 2.1(b) closure, 36 CFR §

1.5(c) requires a "written determination" laying out the "reason(s)" for the

area closure and "an explanation of why less restrictive measures will not

suffice." For any closure resulting "in a significant alteration in the public

use pattern of the park area," the NPS must issue a Federal Rulemaking

under 36 CFR § 1.5(c). After exhaustive search, Defendant has not found

any such written determination or Federal Rulemaking discussing

closure of the old climber's trail.

20. The government seems to rely upon the 2024 Compendium's vague

reference to "revegetation and restoration efforts" as evidence of CFR

compliance (ECF 22 at 2). Furthermore, no restoration efforts—e.g., soil

decompaction, planting, barriers—are evident from photographs of the

old climber's trail (ECF 21-1 at 2-3). By contrast, the 2024 Compendium

includes many explicit area closure maps for restrictions motivated by

wildlife activity, seasonal road closures, boat retrieval / launches, etc.

Even if applicable, "revegetation and restoration efforts" is woefully

inadequate to provide a layperson with notice that an area might be

legally closed, subjecting travelers to misdemeanor liability.1

21. Absent a bona fide area closure under 36 CFR §§ 1.5 and 1.7, the

criminal charge under 36 CFR § 2.1(b) fails.

B. Selective Enforcement Violates Equal Protection

22. Selective enforcement with discriminatory effect and purpose violates the

Fifth Amendment. See U.S. v. Armstrong, 517 U.S. 456, 465 (1996).

1 Should the case not be dismissed, the lack of visible revegetation and restoration efforts on the old climber's trail is why Defendant's expert witness is so essential.

23. Effect: Defendant has produced clear, affirmative evidence of consistent

public use of the allegedly closed trail, such as 57 users posting 83 runs

on Strava (ECF 32-2), four unflagged speed records including one in

2022 on Fastest Known Time (ECF 32-4), and a public YouTube video

with 711K views published by a Colorado resident (ECF 32-7). PACER

searches and community outreach returned no citations.

24. Purpose: Public NPS statements (ECF 32-6) and Bellino's animus

suggest targeting based on Defendant's identity, activity and speech—

collectively protected under U.S. const. amend. I. and V. See Wayte; see

also Steele.

25. This evidence of animus, bias, and inconsistent enforcement in violation

of Defendant's protected rights under U.S. const. amend. I. and V.

mandates dismissal.

C. Inadequate Notice Violates Due Process

26. The Fifth Amendment prohibits the "depriv[ation] of life, liberty or

property, without due process of law." U.S. const. amend. V. While

"'ignorance of the law will not excuse,'" due process invokes "the

requirement of notice." Lambert v. People of the State of California, 355

U.S. 225, 228 (1957) (overturning a vague registration law) (quoting

Shevlin-Carpenter Co. v. State of Minn., 218 U.S. 57, 68 (1910)); see also

Johnson v. U.S., 576 U.S. 591, 595 (2015) (finding unconstitutional a

vague law that failed to "give ordinary people fair notice").

27. Signage for the "old climber's trail" was woefully inadequate—small,

vague, and lacking CFR references. ECF 21-1 at 9-10. The sign from

Defendant's direction of entry states "short cutting causes erosion"

placed next to the trail, camouflage in a sagebrush plant, which most

passing hikers would miss. Even if a passerby noticed the sign, it

provides no indication of a prohibited pathway.

28. The NPS clearly has the resources and ability to make signs visible—e.g.,

the Delta Lake trail, an unofficial trail yet open to parkgoers, has a large,

highly visible sign (ECF 21-1 at 9-11). The NPS also has the resources to

publish maps—e.g., the 2024 Compendium includes many detailed area

closure maps, such as seasonal road closures (snow clearing);

Prospector's Mountain winter closure (bighorn sheep); Antelope Flats

(sage grouse); and Willow Flats (nesting bald eagles) to name a few. The

NPS maintains a separate detailed wildlife closure map and designations

online (https://nps.gov/grte/learn/management/wildlife-closures.htm).

29. Meanwhile, third-party maps showed the trail as open (ECF 32-5),

consistent public use went uncharged (ECF 32-2), similar social trails

are explicitly allowed (ECF 21-1 at 9-10)—including those causing far

greater impact, e.g., Delta Lake trail—and multiple uncited speed records

using the trail were displayed without flag or closure notice (ECF 32-4).

30. NPS's endorsement of Delta Lake in particular, use of which causes far

greater land impact, challenges any claim of risk or damage justifying a

misdemeanor charge for use of the old climber's trail. See Earth

Trekkers, Ultimate Guide to Delta Lake Hike

(https://earthtrekkers.com/surprise-amphitheater-delta-lake-hike)

(showing near-identical terrain to the old climber's trail). Furthermore,

the NPS explicitly allows, and even encourages, activities that require off-

trail travel, such as climbing, hunting, fishing, and wildlife viewing /

photography (see https://nps.gov/grte/planyourvisit).

31. To dispel this consistent, ongoing use, the government could have

installed clear and prominent signage, issued a map in the

Compendium—as the NPS does for many other area closures in Grand

Teton National Park—filed a press release, etc. Instead, the NPS is using

criminal charges to provide closure notice, a violation of Defendant's due

process rights under U.S. const. amend. V.

D. Unconstitutional Delegation of Legislative Power

32. The Constitution vests "[a]ll legislative Powers" in Congress, prohibiting

delegation of "powers which are strictly and exclusively legislative," such

as defining crimes. U.S. Const., art. I, § 1. Only Congress can create

criminal laws and, in doing so, must "write statutes that give ordinary

people fair warning about what the law demands of them." U.S. v. Davis,

588 U.S. 445, 447 (2019) (determining a violent felon statute to be

unconstitutionally vague).

33. In the 1916 Organic Act, Congress delegated authority to the Secretary of

the Interior under 16 U.S.C. § 3 (recodified to 54 U.S.C. § 100751(a)) to

"make and publish such rules and regulations as he may deem

necessary or proper" for national parks, in the early 1900s wild-west era

of poaching and wanton resource destruction. See Paul D. Berkowitz,

U.S. Rangers, The Law of the Land: The History of Law Enforcement in the

Federal Land Management Agencies 18-25 (CT Publ'g Co. n.d.). Through

the criminal statute 18 USC 1865(a), Congress criminalized violation of

any regulation promulgated under the Organic Act's broad mandate. 36

CFR § 2.1(b) and its criminal penalties are authorized by that single

sentence from 1916, which allows unelected officials to criminalize a

wide array of conduct, including running on a trail.

34. 16 U.S.C. § 3 provides no "intelligible principle" to constrain the

Secretary's power, merely offering vague aspirational goals to "conserve"

and "provide for the enjoyment" of parks (recodified to 54 U.S.C. §

100101(a)), allowing the NPS to "roam at will," inventing crimes like trail

shortcutting that Congress never proscribed and which, in this case, are

not even reflected in the NPS's own regulations. See Consumers'

Research v. FCC, 109 F.4th 743, 760 (5th Cir. 2024). This "standardless

sweep" undermines self-government and due process. Kolender v.

Lawson, 461 U.S. 352, 358 (1983).

35. This delegation's severity is heightened by criminal penalties—up to six

months' imprisonment, $5,000 fines, and five years of probation under

18 U.S.C. § 1865(a)—potentially requiring even "more than an intelligible

principle" to survive scrutiny. Touby v. United States, 500 U.S. 160, 165-

66 (1991). Absent such limits, 36 CFR § 2.1(b), as applied via vague

signage and Compendium language, violates Article I § 1, rendering the

criminal charge against Defendant unconstitutional.

36. A lawsuit was filed on Feb. 24, 2025 by the New Civil Liberties Alliance

challenging the constitutionality of NPS application of the Organic Act in

the U.S. District Court for the Southern District of Texas. See BASE

Access v. NPS, No. 4:25-cv-790 (S.D. Tex. 2024).

IV. CONCLUSION

The government (A) cannot prove a valid closure; the prosecution reflects

(B) selective enforcement and (C) inadequate notice violating Fifth Amendment

rights; and (D) 36 CFR § 2.1(b) stems from an unconstitutional delegation of

legislative power. Dismissal with prejudice is warranted.
